How to write copy that sells without sounding like a salesperson

By Estéfani Torres

There's a reason so much copy sounds the same: it borrows from the same templates that circulate in cheap digital marketing courses. "Tired of X? Discover the solution that will change your life!" It's off-putting, it feels plastic, and worse — it doesn't sell.

Copywriting that actually works has one defining trait: it doesn't feel like copywriting. It feels like an honest conversation with someone who genuinely understands what you're going through.

The PAS framework (the human version)

PAS stands for Problem, Agitation, Solution. Most people apply it wrong — like a sales script. The version that actually works looks like this:

  • Problem: Name the pain using the customer's own words, not yours.
  • Agitation: Show that you understand the real consequences (without exaggerating).
  • Solution: Present your offer as a bridge, not a rescue.

Bad example (cliché)

"Tired of not having enough clients? Struggling because your business isn't growing? Discover the secret method that's revolutionizing marketing!"

Good example (human)

"You've been posting every day for two years. You have followers, sure — but you're not sure any of them actually buy. Every Monday you start from zero. Sounds exhausting, right? The problem isn't what you post: it's that you're missing a strategy that connects each post to an actual sale."

The difference: the second one doesn't sound like marketing. It sounds like an honest conversation. And that's exactly why it works.

The basic rules of human copy

1. Use your customer's words, not yours

If your customer says "I don't have time to think about marketing," your copy should use that exact phrase, not "acquisition process optimization." How to get those words: record real conversations with clients and transcribe their exact phrasing.

2. Specific beats aspirational

"More sales" is vague. "12 extra closed deals this month" is specific. The human brain prefers the concrete over the abstract, every time.

3. One idea per sentence

Every sentence should carry exactly one idea. If you need two commas and a "that" in the middle, split it into two sentences.

4. Present tense, active voice

"We design websites that convert" beats "Websites are designed by us with a conversion-oriented approach."

5. Read your copy out loud

If you stumble, it's wrong. If it sounds human, it's right. It's the simplest test — and the least used.

Quick exercise Grab your last Instagram caption. Read it out loud. Does it sound like you talking to a friend? If not — rewrite it until it does.

Typical mistakes that kill copy

  • Empty adjectives: "innovative," "disruptive," "unique." If you can't prove it, don't say it.
  • Stock phrases: "thinking of you," "committed to quality," "your satisfaction is our priority." Everyone uses them, nobody believes them.
  • Talking about yourself, not your customer: "we're a company with 10 years of experience..." Nobody cares until you've shown them why they should.
  • Feature lists without benefits: "includes 12 posts" vs. "12 posts designed so you have a full month of content without lifting a finger."

Copy with a distinct voice

Every brand has a local, cultural voice that's worth protecting. Don't copy the cold, corporate tone you see everywhere, and don't copy the plastic hype of generic marketing gurus either. Use your own voice with pride.

"Let's talk" sounds more genuine than "Request a free consultation." "We'll help you" sounds warmer than "We provide assistance." Small differences, big impact.

Closing thought

Copy that sells is copy that doesn't feel like selling. It's honest, specific, warm and useful. If you read it back and it feels like a conversation with someone who understands what you're living, it's working.
