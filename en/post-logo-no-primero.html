Why your logo shouldn't be the first thing you design

By Estéfani Torres

If I told you the logo is the last thing designed in a solid branding process, you'd probably be surprised. But that's exactly how it works. The logo isn't the starting point — it's the visual consequence of strategic decisions made long before.

When someone tells me "I need a logo for my brand," what I hear is "I need help building a brand." And that gap is exactly why so many small brands feel generic, interchangeable, or worse — invisible.

The right order (in short)

  1. Strategy: who are you, who do you exist for, what do you promise?
  2. Positioning: how do you differentiate from direct competitors?
  3. Voice and tone: how do you speak?
  4. Visual system: typography, palette, photography, illustration.
  5. Logo: the signature of the system you already built.
  6. Applications: mockups, stationery, social media, packaging.

When you start at step 5, you end up with a logo that communicates nothing because there's nothing behind it. It's a symbol floating in a vacuum, waiting for someone to assign it meaning by osmosis. It doesn't work that way.

The most common mistake

Here's how it usually goes: you start a business, open Instagram, and your first instinct is "I need a logo." You pay a freelancer $50 on Fiverr, get something "pretty," put it in your bio and... nothing happens. Because the logo was never the problem — it's a symptom of missing strategy.

"A beautiful logo on a brand with no strategy is like a gala dress on someone who doesn't know where they're going."

What doing it right looks like

Step 1 — Strategy

Before opening Illustrator (or Canva, or any tool), we answer seven questions with the client:

  • Why does this brand exist? (real purpose, not marketing copy)
  • Who is it for? (an ideal customer, not vague demographics)
  • What problem does it solve?
  • Who are the competitors, and what do they look like?
  • What promise does the brand make?
  • What archetype does it embody? (creator, sage, lover, hero...)
  • How does it want to be felt, not just seen?

Step 2 — Voice and tone

We define how the brand speaks. Is it casual or formal? Playful or serious? Does it talk down to its audience or speak to it as an equal? This shapes every word you'll write for the next five years.

Step 3 — Visual system

Before the logo, we define the system. Editorial typography or modern sans-serif? Warm or cool palette? Documentary-style photography or product shots? Once the system is clear, the logo almost designs itself, because it already has a home to live in.

Step 4 — Logo

Now, finally. But the logo isn't ONE drawing — it's a system: primary mark, alternates, monochrome version, icon, reduced version. Each variant has a specific use case (favicon, watermark, social media, stationery).

Exceptions

Are there cases where it makes sense to start with the logo? Yes, but only a few:

  • If you already have 5+ years of an established brand and just need a refresh.
  • If you need something provisional to validate an MVP in a week.
  • If it's a personal project with no commercial ambition.

If you're building something serious that you want to last, don't skip the steps. The logo will be better, will last longer, and will communicate from minute one.

The 3-second test

Once you finish your identity, ask a stranger to look at it for 3 seconds. Then ask: "what do you think this brand sells, and to whom?" If they guess correctly, your system works. If not — go back to step 1.
